Choosing What to Work On

Choosing what to work on is the judgment of selecting problems whose importance, evidence, timing, and fit justify sustained attention.

The current synthesis starts from James Cowling’s Peterman Pod discussion. The sharper second-pass reading is that work should be selected around the underlying “why”: the problem that makes the work worth doing at all. Without that why, teams can drift into artificial goals, fashionable architecture, artifact defense, or work chosen mainly because it creates a promotion story.

The user’s note makes this a personal operating theme: choose work because the problem matters, not because the surrounding status signal is attractive.

Core Frame

Good work sits at the intersection of:

  • Problem importance: the work addresses real user, customer, system, or organizational pain.
  • Evidence: the pain appears in customer conversations, production failures, repeated workflow friction, adoption pull, or clear market need.
  • Timing: some technical, organizational, or market change makes the problem possible or urgent now.
  • Fit: the problem matches the person’s skills, interests, and learning edge.
  • Local value: the surrounding team or company values the problem enough for the work to matter.
  • Opportunity cost: the work is a better use of scarce people than plausible alternatives.

This is narrower than “follow your passion.” It is also narrower than “do what the company asks.” The useful question is whether personal interest, external need, and local value overlap.

Signals of Work Worth Choosing

A real problem, not an artifact to defend. Cowling’s Magic Pocket example supports Problem-Oriented Engineering Teams: a team should own storage, not the continued existence of one storage system. When the problem changes, the work may be to simplify, replace, validate, or delete the artifact. The strong test is opportunity cost: is this the best thing this team could do with its scarce people?

Customer or user pain. Marc Brooker says important engineering problems come from customer pain, technical change, and broader shifts in the world. Customer conversations, on-call work, and postmortems reveal where users spend too much time, where abstractions do not fit, and where repeated failures suggest a deeper service, library, practice, or architectural change.

Repeated operational patterns. Brooker’s postmortem model is a work-selection mechanism. A good postmortem does not stop at the immediate bug; it looks for recurring classes of failure. When many incidents rhyme, the right work may be a library, service, shared practice, or architecture that prevents the whole class.

A problem that is important, soluble, and fit to the person. Barbara Liskov’s advice to graduate students is a compact research version of the rule: good work requires a problem that matters, can plausibly be solved, and matches the researcher’s skills.

An intersection between interest and employer value. Michael Bolin’s career stories add an alignment rule: understand what you like doing, understand what the employer values, and lean into the intersection. His Buck story is the positive example: Android iteration pain was real, company-critical mobile work made the problem valuable, prior art gave an existence proof, and a scoped prototype let adoption expand from a locally useful improvement.

A real constraint surface. Bjarne Stroustrup argues that language design should start from a real problem, not from wanting to design a language for its own sake. Existing users, hardware, compatibility, implementers, and long-lived code are not distractions from the work; they are part of the problem. A direction that only works for imagined future constraints may not survive long enough to matter.

A reasoned contrarian bet. Mike Stonebraker recommends work that is not simply going with the flow. Bryan Cantrill’s Oxide discussion adds the personal version: judge directly and choose work whose appeal is intrinsic rather than just accepting whatever the market currently calls hot or dead. But contrarian conviction still needs contact with user, customer, or market reality.

Anti-Signals

Promotion-first scope. John Myles White warns that engineers can end up choosing work because it creates a promotion story, even when they do not believe the resulting system is technically good or durable. Cowling adds the mirror image: simple, inspectable work can be more valuable than complex work even if it is harder to narrate as impressive.

Title or hype as the terminal goal. Cantrill treats titles as downstream of contribution, not as the thing that should define the work. His advice during the AI-era discussion is to distrust both hype and fatalism, and to keep independent judgment about what is worth doing.

Personal interest without local value. Bolin’s Google reflection is a useful warning. Working hard on a personally interesting problem can still be a poor local choice if the company does not value the work enough for it to matter.

Internal convenience as the starting point. Working Backwards warns against beginning with existing assets, current org boundaries, or available technical pieces. Its PR/FAQ mechanism starts from the desired customer outcome and then forces the organization to decide whether it is willing to do the required work.

Symbolic ownership. Separable Single-Threaded Ownership only works when the problem is actually separable enough for a focused owner or team to make progress. If the owner remains dependent on many shared systems, hidden roadmaps, and half-available teams, the “ownership” label can hide the real constraint.

Artificial goals detached from why. Cowling’s transcript reread makes this failure mode explicit. OKRs, executive requests, and architecture ideas can all be useful, but they become dangerous when they replace the underlying problem as the reason for the work.

Execution speed mistaken for work selection. The Anthropic Institute’s When AI Builds Itself makes this sharper in the AI context. If implementation and experiment execution get much cheaper in human time, the scarce question becomes which goals, hypotheses, and results are worth trusting. Faster doing does not remove the need to decide what should be done.

Practical Heuristic

Before choosing a project, team, or technical direction, ask:

  • What underlying problem makes this work exist?
  • What real user, customer, system, or organizational pain proves this problem matters?
  • What technical or market change makes this problem newly solvable or newly urgent?
  • What repeated failures, postmortems, or workflow frictions point toward this problem?
  • Is the problem actually soluble at the available scale and with the available skills?
  • Does this sit at the intersection of what I care about and what the local environment values?
  • Does the work create leverage by improving a repeated workflow, abstraction, reliability pattern, or decision loop?
  • Can the first step be made concrete enough that others can evaluate it?
  • Would the work still look worthwhile if it produced less title, headcount, or hype value?
  • Does the team or organization have enough separability and support for the work to succeed?
  • Will I stay close enough to users, production, or adoption to learn whether the choice was good?

Relation to Existing Threads

This page is adjacent to Engineering Career Growth, but it is narrower. Career growth asks how an engineer develops and advances. Choosing what to work on asks what problems deserve attention in the first place.

It is also adjacent to Big-Tech Promotion Culture. Promotion literacy is not the enemy; the failure mode is letting the promotion story replace the problem as the unit of judgment.

For AI-Era Engineering Apprenticeship, this page supplies a target for apprenticeship. If AI changes the shape of execution work, developing engineers still need practice choosing and owning real problems that matter.

Recursive Self-Improvement is the frontier-lab version of this pressure. If AI systems approach the ability to improve their successors, problem selection, verification, and governance become more important rather than less.

  • When is a contrarian problem a good bet, and when is it just isolation from useful feedback?
