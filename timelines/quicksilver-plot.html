Quicksilver Partial Plot

This timeline covers Book One of Quicksilver and the first part of Book Two, King of the Vagabonds, through the end of Paris / WINTER OF 1684-1685 (immediately before Amsterdam / APRIL 1685). Later Book Two material is not ingested.

1655

  • Enoch Root visits Grantham and observes the schoolboy Isaac Newton after hearing about him from the apothecary Clarke.

1661-1662

  • Daniel Waterhouse arrives at Trinity College, Cambridge after witnessing Hugh Peters’s execution with his father Drake.
  • The Earl of Upnor kills a Puritan scholar at Trinity; Daniel sees enough to know what happened but fails to bring the matter to justice.
  • Daniel meets Isaac Newton, another alienated Puritan student with a secret interest in natural philosophy.
  • Newton undergoes an intense Whitsunday repentance and begins listing his sins; Daniel later concludes that Newton stopped believing himself capable of sin.

1663-1665

  • Newton performs dangerous optical experiments on his own eye; Daniel assists.
  • Newton studies sundials, colors, Cartesian analysis, and early fluxions.
  • Daniel and Newton go to Stourbridge Fair for prisms and encounter plague.

1665 (Jack Shaftoe / East London)

  • Mother Shaftoe turns Dick, Bob, and Jack Shaftoe out of her Isle of Dogs household to find bread or money.
  • The Shaftoe boys enter the mudlark world around the Thames. Dick dies during an anchor-stealing attempt from a Dutch ship, leaving Bob and Jack marked by guilt and nightmares.
  • John Cole, an older mudlark, is caught and sentenced to hang at Tyburn. Jack and Bob agree to hang from his legs to hasten his death.
  • The boys turn this into a grim trade: they visit Newgate’s condemned prisoners, perform a theatrical advertisement about bad hangings, and sell their services as execution ballast.

1665-1666

  • Daniel spends the plague period between Drake’s London house, Wilkins and Hooke at Epsom, and Newton at Woolsthorpe.
  • With Wilkins, Daniel works on the Universal Character / Philosophical Language.
  • With Hooke, Daniel observes experiments involving microscopy, air, gravity, thermometers, and animal anatomy.
  • At Woolsthorpe, Daniel assists Newton’s prism experiments using Venus as a point source and hears Newton’s ideas about orbital motion and gravity.
  • The Great Fire of London kills Drake Waterhouse after King Charles II orders Drake’s house destroyed as part of a firebreak.

1670

  • Daniel follows Newton in London and discovers Newton’s connection to Monsieur LeFebure, the Earl of Upnor, and alchemical materials.
  • Daniel presents Newton’s reflecting telescope at a Royal Society meeting.
  • The Royal Society considers experiments, letters, instruments, anatomical reports, Hooke’s bubble level, and Wilkins’s Philosophical Language.
  • Daniel learns more about goldsmith banking, coinage, bullion, and the Waterhouse family’s post-Fire real estate ambitions.
  • Enoch Root demonstrates phosphorus at court; the demonstration becomes socially dangerous when the Earl of Upnor is embarrassed.
  • Newton returns to Cambridge with alchemical books and apparatus, including a valuable volume from Upnor.

Late Summer-September 1683

  • Jack is living in Strasbourg after moving through fake soldiering, plague-house squatting, and opportunistic work around the Palatinate.
  • News of the Ottoman siege of Vienna and the good grain harvest draws Jack east with barley merchants, armies, and camp followers.
  • Jack sees John Sobieski and the Winged Hussars cross the Danube and joins an Austrian regiment as a musketeer.
  • After the relief of Vienna, Jack wanders through the Ottoman camp’s loot and accepts custody of a captured Turkish warhorse later called Turk.
  • Chasing an ostrich for its valuable plumes, Jack follows it into a Turkish mine under Vienna and interrupts the execution of women from the Grand Vizier’s harem.
  • Jack kills or disables the Janissaries, captures the Damascus-steel saber, kills the ostrich, and rescues Eliza.
  • Jack and Eliza leave Vienna in improvised disguise, arguing their way into a partnership: he supplies mobility and protection; she promises to handle money.

Autumn 1683

  • On the road from Vienna, Eliza recounts her Qwghlmian childhood, Barbary capture, harem training, languages, and the still-unidentified powerful “Personage” from the pirate ship.
  • Jack tries to sell the ostrich plumes quickly in Linz, but Eliza persuades him that they should wait for the Leipzig fair and sell into the proper luxury market.
  • In devastated Bohemia, Jack explains the Devil’s Poor and the Vagabond world as a society of outcasts, informants, deserters, performers, smugglers, and escaped serfs.
  • Jack uses gunpowder to stun fish in a pond, feeding a large Vagabond camp and drawing out information about hot springs where he and Eliza might survive winter.

Winter 1683-1684

  • Jack and Eliza winter near sulfurous hot springs in Bohemia, living in a repaired hut and slowly moving from survival partnership into sexual and emotional entanglement.
  • Eliza pushes Jack to tell stories. Jack recounts how he and Bob came under the notice of Sir Winston Churchill and young John Churchill, and how Bob served more honorably than Jack at Maestricht.
  • Jack’s Maestricht story links John Churchill, the Duke of Monmouth, and D’Artagnan, while also exposing Jack’s own instinct to loot rather than pursue military glory.
  • Eliza sews Turkish silk into presentable clothing, transforming loot into social costume for Leipzig.

Late April 1684

  • Jack and Eliza enter Leipzig during the fair and discover its tightly organized world of trading houses, money-changers, warehouses, Bourse activity, booksellers, and luxury goods.
  • Eliza recognizes that goods, coins, mine shares, credit, and information can be converted into one another. Jack experiences this as a kind of liquefaction or quicksilver market logic.
  • They meet Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, called the Doctor, at the fair. He explains binary numbers, relates them to Wilkins’s cryptographic work, and invites them to inspect Brunswick-Luneburg silver mines.
  • Leibniz submits a long-delayed mathematical manuscript to the Acta Eruditorum, knowing that only he and Newton currently understand the method and that Newton may have invented an earlier version.
  • Leibniz’s quarrel with booksellers is comic but revealing: he is brilliant, abrasive, ambitious, and short of usable patronage and cash.

Harz Mountains / Walpurgisnacht 1684

  • Leibniz takes Jack and Eliza to the Harz mines, where his wind-powered drainage machinery and other improvements are meant to revive silver production.
  • In an abandoned mine, Leibniz shows fossil evidence and argues from strata, shells, and bones that the mountain preserves traces of ancient life and former waters.
  • In Bockboden, Jack meets Enoch Root in an apothecary’s shop. Enoch advises iron-rich food for Eliza’s blood loss and later teaches Jack how quicksilver is used to extract silver from low-grade ore.
  • Jack witnesses a witch-burning in Bockboden, gets lost in the woods, eats hallucinogenic stew, and stumbles into a Walpurgisnacht gathering on the mountain.
  • Mistaken for a Watcher, Jack flees the crowd and ends up in the mine during Eliza’s investor performance. His apparent offer to descend into danger in her place helps impress the investors.

Summer 1684

  • Jack and Eliza travel west through Hanover and into the Dutch Republic. In Hanover, Leibniz lets them sleep near the library and teaches Eliza a binary-based cipher.
  • Amsterdam appears as “the Place”: a city where ships, canals, warehouses, markets, coffee houses, and the Exchange make commerce continuous and global.
  • Eliza observes the Amsterdam trading crowd, reads rumor and price movement, and begins corresponding with Leibniz. Jack learns the city by wandering, workhouse risk, canal labor, dockyards, and sailor-press danger.
  • Enoch’s quicksilver lesson gives Eliza a strategic idea: the mines depend on mercury supply, and whoever controls quicksilver has leverage.
  • Jack leaves Amsterdam as a courier carrying a banker letter to Signor Cozzi in Paris. He uses speed, horses, and underworld contacts to convert travel itself into profit.

Winter 1684-1685 / Early 1685

  • In Paris, Jack reconnects with St.-George, a rat-catcher and underworld broker, and lodges with the Armenian Esphahnian coffee family.
  • The Esphahnians’ coffee story connects Marseille, Mocha, Spanish silver, and shipwrecked capital, reinforcing the book’s larger money-and-commerce system.
  • Jack sells ostrich plumes and repeatedly carries messages between Paris and Lyons for Genoese bankers. One Lyon errand reveals that Charles II has died and James II now rules England.
  • In the Hague, Eliza meets Jean Antoine de Mesmes, comte d’Avaux, while skating. He introduces her to Mary of Orange and the Duke of Monmouth, and uses her to wound Mary emotionally.
  • Eliza also works with Gomer Bolstrood on lead, saltpeter, gunpowder, and a still-hidden anti-James II project tied to slavery, Protestant politics, and the English succession.
  • Jack encounters Monsieur Arlanc, a Huguenot contact, during the dangerous Paris-Lyons route and learns more about selling horses and moving through France.
  • Returning toward Paris from Lyons, Jack suffers an elaborate Paris hallucination in which graves, skeletons, galley-bound Huguenots, priests, fishwives, and quicksilver-animated stone creatures turn Paris into a musical argument about sin, predestination, confession, and grace.
  • Jack then interrupts a French military procession near the Place Royale, apparently trying to demonstrate Turk’s value or force recognition. John Churchill recognizes him, blocks a sword-thrust that would likely have killed him, and warns that silence is Jack’s only chance of survival.

October-November 1713

  • Enoch Root arrives in Boston, meets young Ben Franklin, and finds Daniel at the Massachusetts Bay Colony Institute of Technologickal Arts.
  • Enoch delivers Princess Caroline’s request that Daniel return to England to help with the Newton-Leibniz dispute.
  • Daniel leaves Faith and Godfrey in Massachusetts and boards Minerva.
  • Minerva struggles to get clear of the New England coast.
  • In Plymouth Bay, local pirate craft attempt to close on Minerva. Dappa and van Hoek use captured pirates, feigned weakness, and Daniel as a false captain in a tactical deception.
  • Edward Teach’s larger pirate flotilla appears in Plymouth Bay.
  • Minerva escapes Plymouth and enters Cape Cod Bay with Teach’s fleet in pursuit.
  • Daniel descends to the hold and spends an hour smashing van Hoek’s obsolete Malabar china into blunderbuss ammunition. Dappa reveals Minerva was built on the Malabar coast (not in Amsterdam).
  • Dappa explains pirate incentive structure: individual captains race to take the prize alone, since the lion’s share goes to the ship that boards the prize. This causes the fleet to fragment.
  • Dappa introduces the concept of Zog (Dutch for “wake,” from zuigen, “to suck”): a heavily loaded pirate schooner has enormous Zog and is thus slower than it appears.
  • Minerva fights off multiple pirate vessels in sequence: scatters caltrops and flying china-shards onto a sloop; destroys a whaleboat; rams and sinks the burned sloop amidships.
  • Van Hoek readies carronnades (multi-ton blunderbusses) loaded with chains, grapeshot, and redundant ironmongery; fires them at a ketch converging from leeward, wrecking it.
  • Daniel, watching from the stern cabin windows, is hit by flying glass and buckshot from the ketch’s dying shot. He is sewn up by the ship’s surgeon on the upperdeck.
  • Teach’s flagship sends a flag of truce demanding Daniel specifically. Van Hoek refuses: he nails his flag to the mast (signaling he will not strike colors or negotiate), then raises every sail.
  • Daniel — calm despite Blackbeard’s pursuit, because chaos frightens him more than death does — applies celestial-mechanics trigonometry (using Provincetown church-steeple and Cape Cod landmarks as fixed references) to confirm the pursuing schooner is leewardly. The schooner runs aground at Race Point, Cape Cod’s northernmost tip.
  • A tacking duel plays out off Race Point between Minerva and Teach’s sloop. Minor exchanges of fire; neither side lands a decisive blow.
  • Dappa persuades van Hoek that out-sailing Teach is sweeter revenge than out-fighting him. Minerva spreads full sail — studdingsails nearly doubling her yard width — and escapes into the Atlantic as the sun sets on America.
  • Teach’s Queen Anne’s Revenge, overcrowded with boarders and under-victualled for a November North Atlantic passage, cannot follow.
  • Minerva runs large before a quartering wind toward England. Book One ends.
