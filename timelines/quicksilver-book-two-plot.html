Quicksilver Book Two Plot

This timeline covers Book Two of Quicksilver, King of the Vagabonds.

1665

  • Jack Shaftoe, Bob Shaftoe, and Dick Shaftoe grow up as East London mudlarks around the Isle of Dogs.
  • Dick dies during an attempted anchor theft from a Dutch vessel. Jack and Bob are left with guilt, nightmares, and a practical education in theft.
  • After John Cole is arrested and sent to Newgate, Jack and Bob turn execution assistance into a service for condemned prisoners, learning how crowds, punishment, commerce, and theater fit together.

Late Summer 1683

  • Jack drifts through plague houses, fake soldiering, smuggling, and Continental war.
  • The Ottoman siege of Vienna draws soldiers, merchants, camp followers, artillery, horses, and looters east.
  • Jack joins the Christian relief of Vienna for opportunity more than piety.

September 1683

  • After the Ottoman defeat, Jack loots the Grand Vizier’s camp.
  • Jack pursues valuable ostrich plumes, captures the Turkish warhorse Turk, and follows the trail into a mine.
  • In the mine, Jack interrupts the execution of harem captives and rescues Eliza.
  • Eliza explains her Qwghlmian origin, Barbary captivity, harem education, and the unresolved powerful Personage from her past.
  • Eliza redirects Jack away from cheap resale and toward Leipzig, where luxury goods and financial instruments can be converted into more durable value.

Autumn 1683

  • Jack and Eliza flee through devastated Bohemia.
  • The landscape turns the Thirty Years’ War into present-tense social fact: ruined settlements, religious fracture, hunger, wandering people, and opportunistic violence.
  • Jack uses food, performance, and shared outsider status to extract route intelligence from vagabonds.

Winter 1683-1684

  • Jack and Eliza winter near hot springs.
  • Their sexual and emotional partnership deepens, while Jack narrates his past with Bob, John Churchill, Monmouth, Maestricht, and D’Artagnan.
  • Eliza learns that Jack’s apparent chaos is tied to guilt, class humiliation, appetite, and trauma.

April 1684

  • Jack and Eliza reach Leipzig.
  • Eliza sees how silk, plumes, coin, Kuxen, credit, and information become mutually convertible at the fair.
  • Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz appears as “the Doctor,” selling mining shares while preparing his calculus work for publication.
  • The Leipzig booksellers’ world links publication, reputation, priority, and the intellectual infrastructure behind the later Newton-Leibniz dispute.

Late April 1684

  • Jack and Eliza leave Leipzig with Leibniz’s caravan.
  • Leibniz’s mining scheme shows that geology, machinery, capital, rumor, and court patronage all have to work together before silver can become money.
  • Eliza keeps moving between commercial agent, sexual target, social performer, and potential correspondent.

Walpurgisnacht 1684

  • In the Harz mining world, Enoch Root gives Jack a practical lesson in quicksilver and silver extraction.
  • Leibniz’s machinery and Enoch’s mercury knowledge make mining a system of natural philosophy, labor, capital, and alchemy.
  • Jack’s Walpurgisnacht mine adventure turns quicksilver into hallucination, danger, and unstable symbolic material.

Summer 1684

  • Jack and Eliza approach Amsterdam, “The Place.”
  • Amsterdam appears as a conversion machine: warehouses, canals, ships, labor, VOC shares, bills, rumors, letters, and coffee-house talk all circulate.
  • Eliza belongs in this market world more naturally than Jack.
  • Jack leaves Amsterdam for Paris as a courier, trying to turn Turk and the plumes into money.

Winter 1684-1685

  • Jack moves through the Dutch Republic, Spanish Netherlands, France, Paris, and Lyons through horse brokers, Armenian coffee networks, bankers, couriers, and police attention.
  • Paris blocks Jack’s plans because the horse and luxury markets are controlled by class access and brokers.
  • Jack finds Monsieur Arlanc among Huguenot galley slaves and learns that an individual escape can endanger the whole chained group.
  • Exhaustion, illness, hunger, religious fear, sex, shame, and quicksilver imagery produce Jack Shaftoe’s Paris Hallucination.
  • Churchill recognizes Jack during a royal-military spectacle and prevents his immediate death.

February 1685

  • In the Hague, Eliza enters diplomatic and succession politics.
  • Jean Antoine de Mesmes, comte d’Avaux introduces Eliza to Mary of Orange, the Duke of Monmouth, Gomer Bolstrood, and anti-James actors.
  • Eliza begins to see that procurement of lead, saltpeter, gunpowder, shares, letters, and rumors can make rebellion and market speculation part of the same system.

April-May 1685

  • Eliza’s Amsterdam opera scenes convert Monmouth’s plans, French aristocratic performance, Sluys, VOC trading, d’Arcachon, and market expectations into political economy.
  • Jack is trapped by d’Arcachon-linked power in Paris and punished through humiliation and display.
  • Churchill gives Jack a narrow path out of immediate death, but not out of coercive power.
  • Jack returns to Amsterdam with Yevgeny and plunder from France.
  • Mr. Vliet and Mr. Foot convert Jack’s risk into maritime finance aboard God’s Wounds.

June 1685

  • D’Avaux escorts Eliza out of Amsterdam toward France.
  • William of Orange’s men intercept the movement, and William asks Eliza to become useful inside Versailles.
  • Eliza is caught between French state power and Dutch anti-French strategy.
  • Off the coast of Europe and North Africa, Jack’s voyage aboard God’s Wounds collapses into mutiny, Barbary attack, and capture.
  • Jack’s attempt to create a final heroic reputation fails because the crew cannot understand him. The book ends with Jack chained to a galley oar.
