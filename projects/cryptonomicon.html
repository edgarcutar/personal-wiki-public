Cryptonomicon

Author: Neal Stephenson
Published: 1999
Genre: Historical fiction / techno-thriller / cryptography
Structure: Dual timeline — WWII (1940–1945) and the late 1990s internet era

“Engrossing … insightful … fascinating and often hysterical… Cryptonomicon is really three novels in one, featuring healthy portions of World War II adventure, cryptography, and high-tech finance, with treasure hunting thrown in for good measure.”
— USA Today

“A hell of a read.” — Wired

Summary

Two interlocking storylines separated by fifty years. In WWII, Lawrence Pritchard Waterhouse (mathematician, US Navy) and Marine Raider Bobby Shaftoe are assigned to the ultra-secret Detachment 2702, whose mission is to protect the Allied secret that they have broken the German Enigma code — by staging elaborate cover operations so the Nazis never suspect. Alongside them is a Japanese engineer, Goto Dengo, who later becomes entangled in the burial of Nazi gold in the Philippines.

In the 1990s, Lawrence’s grandson Randy Waterhouse and his business partner Avi Halaby are building Epiphyte Corporation — a telecom venture targeting overseas Filipino contract workers — which eventually evolves into a plan for a data haven in the fictional Southeast Asian sultanate of Kinakuta: a place where encrypted data can be stored free of government surveillance. Bobby Shaftoe’s granddaughter Amy Shaftoe joins Randy to salvage a sunken Nazi submarine whose cargo holds a key to the Arethusa cipher and the Nazi gold.

The two timelines converge around a Nazi cipher called Arethusa, the buried gold, and the ongoing relevance of the codebreaking secret to living characters.

Lawrence Waterhouse — Origin

From the source (early chapters):

  • Born in West Point, Virginia; father Godfrey Waterhouse IV was a Congregationalist-turned-Classics professor; mother Alice Pritchard Waterhouse (died of pneumonia when Lawrence was young)
  • Grew up in Moorhead, Minnesota with grandparents Bunyan and Blanche Waterhouse
  • Taught himself Bach on the organ at age five; later worked on a pipe organ repair that revealed to him the mathematical architecture beneath music (“the heavens were riven open”)
  • Iowa State College (worked on the Atanasoff-Berry computing machine with J.V. Atanasoff and Clifford Berry); then Princeton on a Congregational scholarship
  • At Princeton’s Fine Hall/IAS, met Alan Turing — initially while looking for help on a sprocket-tooth optimization problem — and began a lasting intellectual friendship
  • Turing made a sexual proposal; Lawrence “decided he was probably one of those people” who wasn’t interested; they remained friends without awkwardness
  • Third member of their bicycle-riding group: Rudy, a German (Alan and Rudy had a closer relationship)

Bobby Shaftoe — Origin

From the source (early chapters and Shanghai scenes):

  • From Oconomowoc, Wisconsin; uncle Jack Shaftoe (Manila-based businessman and shipper)
  • Fourth Marines, Shanghai; quiet, contemplative drunk; sniper scout; wrote haiku
  • Encountered Goto Dengo at a Japanese sushi restaurant in Shanghai — entered alone on a dare to taste raw fish, caused a brawl over a question about nori (“Are you bullshitting me about the seaweed?”). Goto Dengo wrestled him into paralysis, then corrected him: “It is not seaweed. The English word is maybe calabash?”
  • Sought out Goto Dengo afterward; learned judo; taught Goto to throw properly. Brief genuine cross-cultural friendship before war made them enemies.
  • Arrived in Manila the night of December 7, 1941 (Pearl Harbor), with a bouquet of roses for Glory Pascual in Intramuros. Pearl Harbor happened while they were drinking tea with her parents.

Epiphyte Corporation and the Data Haven

From the source (Randy’s chapter on the Manila flight):

  • Avi observed at Manila’s Ninoy Aquino International Airport that there was a dedicated lane for OCWs (Overseas Contract Workers) — Filipinos working abroad as maids, nurses, singers. This was the insight: a large underserved population with intense need to communicate cheaply with family.
  • Guideline 1: Look for places where population growth + per-capita income are about to “take off the way it did in Nippon, Taiwan, Singapore.”
  • Guideline 2: Pick a technology where they have a competitive edge. In the 1990s, that was networking.
  • The Philippines is “innately hedged”: if the economy stays bad, there are more OCWs who need communication services; if it improves, there’s explosive domestic telecom growth. Either way, opportunity.
  • The venture name for the software trademark they are protecting at one point: “Pinoy-grams” (Randy told not to say it out loud while the trademark is pending).
  • Randy uses Ordo (Novus Ordo Seclorum) encryption software — streaming mode that decrypts and immediately expunges plaintext from disk.

The Arethusa Problem

(From publisher’s description and chapter titles; details of the cipher itself not in sampled pages)

  • Arethusa is an unbreakable Nazi cipher referenced in both timelines.
  • Detachment 2702’s mission in WWII: keep the Nazis ignorant that Enigma has been cracked, by staging cover operations that explain away Allied knowledge without revealing the decryption capability.
  • The modern plot discovers that Arethusa and the sunken submarine hold the key to Nazi gold buried (by Goto Dengo) in the Philippines — and that the secret is entangled with living people who don’t want it revealed.

Key Concepts

  • Detachment 2702: Ultra-secret Allied unit (so secret only Churchill, Roosevelt, and a handful knew it existed); mission: protect the Enigma secret by staging cover operations
  • Station Alpha: US Navy radio intelligence station in Shanghai; its equipment was secretly evacuated to Corregidor before Pearl Harbor (Shaftoe and the Fourth Marines did the loading)
  • Ordo / Novus Ordo Seclorum: Randy’s cryptographic software; streaming decryption that never writes plaintext to disk; named for “New Order” and “Centuries” (it would take Centuries to crack)
  • Solitaire: A card-deck-based cipher invented by Bruce Schneier for the novel; see appendix by Schneier (also: the Perl implementation by Ian Goldberg appears in Enoch Root’s email to Randy)
  • Data haven: Offshore storage of encrypted data, free from government surveillance or censorship; the ultimate goal of the Epiphyte/Kinakuta venture — see Data Haven
  • Athena / Ares / Metis: Enoch Root’s explicit framework for technology, civilization, and violence. Athena represents metis — craft, cunning, and technology — while Ares represents recurring stupid/coercive violence. See Metis

Acknowledgments

From the source:

Bruce Schneier invented Solitaire, graciously consented to my use of it in this novel, and wrote the appendix. Ian Goldberg wrote the Perl script that appears in Enoch Root’s e-mail message to Randy.

Stephenson chose WWII as his “gratitude cutoff date” for acknowledgments — i.e., he only thanks those who contributed after WWII ends.
